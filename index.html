Flood


Floods are when water overflows from the normal boundaries of a stream, river or other body of water, or accumulates in an area that is usually dry. There are two main types of floods. Inundation floods are slow and develop over hours or days. Flash floods occur suddenly, often without warning and usually due to heavy rain. Though annual flooding is a natural phenomenon in many parts of the world, human habitation and land-use practices have led to an increase in frequency and size of floods. Floods are also predicted to become even more frequent and severe in future due to climate change. Floods can be extremely dangerous and cause massive human, environmental and material damage to communities.

Do you really know how to access a plan

1. Access A Plan

Understand your area's flood risks: know the local terrain, water sources and weather patterns and ask authorities about local flood plains and risks. If you live in an area prone to frequent or severe flooding, consider relocating, building elevated storage buildings or using floating shelters. Identify safe evacuation routes in your community and practise evacuation drills. Store any valuables and dangerous materials above likely water levels.

2. Reduce Risk

Build and maintain your home with floods in mind. Try to avoid building within 200 metres of a high-tide coastline or on riverbanks, gullies and flood plains. But if you must, seek expert building advice to build an appropriate foundation and elevate your home. Use flood damage-resistant materials and flood-proof the construction. Maintain water channels outside the home and construct barriers (such as levees, berms or floodwalls) to prevent flood water from entering buildings. Keep supplies such as plastic sheeting, sandbags, sand and shovels to protect your home.

3.Prepeare To Respond

Stay informed: monitor weather information and listen for instructions from local authorities. If advised to evacuate or if you think you are in danger, evacuate immediately as practised. During a flood: "turn around, don't drown"—never try to walk, swim or drive through swift water. Move to higher ground and do not return home until declared safe to do so. After a flood, clean up carefully and pump out water from your home slowly to avoid walls collapsing. Do not drink from, play or swim in flood water and watch out for debris and other hazards such as electrical items or poisonous animals.

Do And Donts During Flood

Monitor Local Weather Conditions and Listen for Official Advice

Regularly check weather updates through reliable sources, such as weather apps, news broadcasts, and government alerts. Stay informed about upcoming storms or heavy rain predictions, which could indicate potential flooding.

Know Your Area's Flood Risks

Understand if your area is prone to flooding by checking local flood maps and resources provided by authorities. Be aware of natural features nearby (like rivers or low-lying areas) that may increase flood risk.

Store Important Documents and Hazardous Materials High Up

Keep essential documents (like identification, insurance papers) in waterproof bags and store them in higher places to prevent water damage. Securely store hazardous materials, such as chemicals or gasoline, away from ground level to avoid spills or contamination in floodwaters.

Identify a Safe Evacuation Route and Practice It

Plan a safe evacuation route to higher ground or a designated shelter in case of severe flooding. Practice this route with family or household members to ensure everyone knows what to do in an emergency.

Avoid Building on Riverbanks, Gullies, or Flood Plains Where Possible

Choose safer, elevated locations for building homes or businesses to reduce the risk of flood damage. If building on flood-prone land is unavoidable, consider elevated structures and flood-resistant materials.

Build and Maintain Your Home with Floods in Mind

Use water-resistant materials and install features like flood vents in flood-prone areas of your home. Regularly inspect and maintain drains, gutters, and the overall foundation to prevent water seepage during floods.

Preparation for the Flood

Before The Flood

Know Your Risk: Find out if you live in a flood-prone area by checking flood maps or asking local authorities.
Create an Emergency Kit: Include essentials like water, non-perishable food, medications, flashlights, batteries, a first-aid kit, and important documents (in waterproof bags).
Make an Evacuation Plan: Know your evacuation route, and plan a safe meeting point if family members get separated.
Store Important Items High Up: Place important documents, electronics, and valuable items on higher shelves to keep them out of reach of floodwaters.
Prepare Your Home:
Install check valves to prevent floodwater from backing up into drains.
Consider using flood-resistant building materials in flood-prone areas.
Clean gutters and drainage systems to allow water to flow away from your home.
Stay Informed: Sign up for weather alerts and pay attention to flood warnings in your area.

During The Flood: Safty measure

Follow Evacuation Orders: If local authorities tell you to evacuate, do so immediately. Floodwaters can rise quickly.
Avoid Walking or Driving Through Floodwaters: Just six inches of water can knock you down, and one foot can sweep a car away.
Move to Higher Ground: If you're unable to evacuate, go to the highest floor of your home but avoid closed spaces like attics if there’s no way out.
Turn Off Utilities: If time permits, turn off electricity, gas, and water to prevent electrical hazards and contamination.

After Flood Recovery

Wait for Official Clearance: Do not return home until authorities say it’s safe.
Avoid Contact with Floodwater: It may contain dangerous debris, chemicals, or sewage.
Document Damage for Insurance: Take photos or videos of any damage to your home and belongings before cleaning up.
Clean and Disinfect: Dry out your home as quickly as possible to prevent mold. Clean surfaces with disinfectants to remove any contamination.
Seek Assistance if Needed: Reach out to local disaster recovery resources or aid organizations if you need support with repairs or other recovery efforts.

Flood Management Programs by Government Of India

Flood Management Programme (FMP)

Description: Initiated by the Ministry of Water Resources, River Development, and Ganga Rejuvenation, the Flood Management Programme (FMP) provides financial assistance to state governments for flood management and anti-erosion measures.
Objective: To reduce flood risks in vulnerable areas by supporting infrastructure development and strengthening flood defenses.
Key Initiatives: Construction and maintenance of embankments, anti-erosion works, river training, and flood protection structures like levees and dikes.
Focus Areas: States with high flood-prone areas such as Assam, Bihar, Uttar Pradesh, and West Bengal.

National Disaster Management Authority (NDMA) - Flood Management Guidelines

Description: NDMA, under the Ministry of Home Affairs, provides guidelines for flood preparedness, mitigation, and response across India. These guidelines help in preparing for floods, improving response measures, and rehabilitating affected areas.
Objective: To create a proactive approach to flood management through preparedness, early warning systems, and efficient disaster response.
Key Initiatives: Promoting community-based flood management, risk assessment, flood forecasting systems, and public awareness programs.

National Flood Control Programme

Description: Established in 1954, this is one of India's earliest flood management programs aimed at controlling and managing floods in vulnerable areas.
Objective: To reduce flood damage through embankment construction, river training, and other engineering measures.
Key Initiatives: Construction of embankments, dams, and reservoirs to store excess water, along with channel improvements to direct water flow away from inhabited areas.

Dam Rehabilitation and Improvement Project (DRIP)

Description: DRIP is a project managed by the Central Water Commission (CWC) with World Bank funding to improve and modernize dams across India.
Objective: To enhance dam safety, reduce the risk of dam-induced flooding, and improve dam infrastructure for effective water management.
Key Initiatives: Repair, maintenance, and strengthening of dams, especially in flood-prone areas, to ensure they can handle high inflows during heavy rains.

Mukhya Mantri Jal Swavlamban Abhiyan (MJSA) - Rajasthan

Description: Though primarily a water conservation initiative, MJSA in Rajasthan aims to improve water retention and reduce flood risks in drought and flood-prone areas.
Objective: To increase groundwater recharge, prevent flash floods, and build resilience in flood-prone areas.
Key Initiatives: Construction of water harvesting structures, desilting of ponds, and community engagement for water management.

Public Awareness and Community-Based Programs

Description: Various states have implemented public awareness campaigns to educate residents in flood-prone areas on how to prepare for floods.
Objective: To improve community resilience, preparedness, and response to floods.
Key Initiatives: Training programs, mock drills, awareness drives in schools, and local partnerships to engage communities in flood preparedness.

Sustainable Catchment Area Treatment and Afforestation Programs

Description: The Indian government promotes catchment area treatment and afforestation programs to reduce soil erosion and control floods in river basins.
Objective: To restore degraded land, reduce runoff, and slow down water flow, thus preventing flash floods and erosion.
Key Initiatives: Planting trees, constructing check dams, and other land management practices in vulnerable areas.

Noteable Measures

Floodplain Zoning Acts: Some states have started implementing floodplain zoning regulations to restrict construction in high-risk flood zones.
Hydro-meteorological Network: India has a vast hydro-meteorological network for collecting rainfall and river data, which helps in accurate flood forecasting.
